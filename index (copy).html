Abstract

Theory for incoherent periodic source Talbot interferometer: A partially coherent Gaussian-Schell model beam propagating through a grating

This chapter investigates the propagation of Gaussian Schell-model (GSM) beams through binary diffraction gratings using the cross-spectral density (CSD) formalism. We derive the full intensity pattern at arbitrary propagation distances, for a single GSM beam through one grating. The intensity pattern behind a periodic GSM source is then investigated. Interactive simulations demonstrate how the intensity pattern depends on the GSM beam, grating and interferometer parameters.

Mathematical Derivation

Cross-Spectral Density Propagation & Intensity Expression

1. The Gaussian Schell-Model Source

A GSM source is characterised by a cross-spectral density (CSD) of Gaussian form in both the intensity profile and the spectral degree of coherence:

$$W^{(0)}(x_1, x_2) = \sqrt{S(x_1)\,S(x_2)}\;\mu^{(0)}(x_1-x_2)$$
Eq. 1
$$W^{(0)}(x_1, x_2) = A^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{x_1^2+x_2^2}{4w_0^2}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_1-x_2)^2}{2\ell_c^2}\right)$$
Eq. 2
Parameters: $w_0$ — beam waist (intensity half-width); $\ell_c$ — transverse coherence length; $\xi = x/d$ — dimensionless transverse coordinate scaled by grating period $d$.

2. Free-Space Propagation via Fresnel Integral

Under the paraxial approximation the CSD propagates as a double Fresnel integral:

$$W(x_1,x_2;z) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2 z^2} \iint W^{(0)}(x_1',x_2')\, t(x_1')\,t^*(x_2')\, e^{\,\mathrm{i}\frac{\pi}{\lambda z}\left[(x_1-x_1')^2-(x_2-x_2')^2\right]} \,\mathrm{d}x_1'\,\mathrm{d}x_2'$$
Eq. 3

3. Binary Grating Transmission & Fourier Expansion

For a binary grating of period $d$ and open fraction $f$, the transmission function is expanded in Fourier harmonics:

$$t(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n\,e^{\,2\pi\mathrm{i} n x/d}, \qquad a_n = f\,\mathrm{sinc}(nf)$$
Eq. 4

4. Full Intensity Expression (Double Sum)

After evaluating the Fresnel integrals with residual wavefront curvature parameter $\beta = R^{-1}(L) - L^{-1}$ (retained to recover Lau-effect cross-terms), the on-axis intensity at propagation distance $z = 2L$ takes the form:

$$I(\xi,\zeta) = \sum_{m,n}\; a_m a_n^*\; \underbrace{e^{\,2\pi\mathrm{i}(m-n)\xi}}_{\text{fringe tilt }T_1} \underbrace{e^{\,-\mathrm{i}\pi(m^2-n^2)\zeta(1+\zeta/\varrho)}}_{\text{Talbot/curvature }T_2} \underbrace{e^{\,-\pi^2(m+n)^2\,\xi^2/\xi_0^2}}_{\text{envelope tilt }T_3} \underbrace{e^{\,-\pi^2(m-n)^2/(2\xi^2)}}_{\text{order attenuation }T_4} \underbrace{e^{\,-2\pi^2(m-n)^2/\xi_c^2}}_{\text{coherence filter }T_5}$$
Eq. 5
$T_1$ — spatial fringe carrier (interference between orders $m$ and $n$).   $T_2$ — Talbot phase with wavefront-curvature correction; self-images occur when $\zeta(1+\zeta/\varrho) \in \mathbb{Z}$.   $T_3$ — Gaussian beam envelope tilt from finite beam waist.   $T_4$ — attenuation of high-order cross-terms.   $T_5$ — coherence filter suppressing orders separated by more than $\xi_c \propto \ell_c/d$.

In general interferometers coherently split a single wave into two or more paths, a perturbation is carried out on one or more of the wavelets, then upon recombination the result of the action on the optical path can be measured. A perturbation can be in many forms, but in general is anything that alters the optical length of a waves path - e.g. change in length of the path, or refractive index. Many metrological results can be encoded into the phase of these wavelets: optical density of a material, relativistic shift of a path length, and of course the gravitational field. Our theoretical consideration of interferometers should start with the underlying principle the superposition of wavefronts \cite{intro_lab_optics}. This has routes in classical wave theory the first realistic conceptualisation of wavefront propagation was made by Christiaan Huygens in 1678. Fresnel later improved on Huygens' wavefront construction by considering interference and first explained diffraction effects with the Huygens-Fresnel principle. Kirchhoff then developed the Kirchhoff integral theorem, which is a more mathematically rigorous formulation of wave propagation and diffraction for scalar fields. \subsection{Huygens wavelets} Huygens principle considered in the \emph{Treatise on Light} `the origin of these waves, and the manner in which they spread'. He argues that `each little region of a luminous body $\ldots$ generates its own waves of which that region is the centre'. He goes onto say that `Similarly the other particles of the sphere $\ldots$ will each make its own wave. But each of these waves can be infinitely feeble' \cite{huygens1912treatise}.\\ \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[width=0.35\linewidth]{Version_2022//ChapterInt_theory/Huygens.png} \label{fig:huygens} \caption{} \end{figure} In a more modern take: Every point on a wavefront, can be considered a source of secondary wavelets.\\ \begin{figure} \centering \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{ChapterInt_theory/huygens.png} \caption{} \label{fig:placeholder} \end{figure} Consider an electric field strength, $E$:\\ \begin{equation} E(x, y, z=0) = A(x, y) \end{equation} If we get the boundary conditions at infinity then the solution must be harmonic and it can immediately be written that \begin{equation} E(x, y, 0) = A(x,y) = E_0 e^{i(k_x x + k_y y)} \end{equation} where $\sum_{i} k_i^2 = \frac{n^2\omega^2}{c^2}$ The wave equation is linear; hence we can integrate over the contributions from different wavelengths to obtain:\\ \begin{equation} A(x, y) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^2} \iint A(k_x,k_y)\mathcal{H}(k_x,k_y) e^{i(k_xx+k_yy)}dk_xdk_y \end{equation} where $\mathcal{H}(k_x,k_y) = e^{iz\sqrt{k^2-k_x^2-k_y^2}}$ \begin{equation} U(r_0) \propto \frac{U_0 e^{ikr_0}}{r_0} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \text{at P: } U(P) = -\frac{i}{d} U(r_0) \int_S \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\kappa(\chi) dS \label{eqn:huygenseqn} \end{equation} where $\kappa(\chi) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos\chi)$. This is an inclination factor not originally considered by Huygens. Its action is to reduce the contribution from highly inclined wavelets, and to prevent the back transmission of forwardly propagating waves. A more mathematically complete picture was later developed by Kirchhoff.\\ \subsection{Kirchhoff's integral theorem} Kirchhoff's integral theorem provides a formal solution using Green's functions. We begin with the Helmholtz wave equation:\\ \begin{equation} \nabla^2U + k^2U = 0 \label{eqn:helmholtzWave} \end{equation} We then introduce a Green's function $G$ satisfying: \begin{equation} \nabla^2 G + k^2 G = -\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\end{equation} whose solution is the outgoing spherical wave: \begin{equation} G(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r}') = \frac{e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}}{4\pi|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} \label{eqn:greensFunc} \end{equation} Applying Green's second identity: \begin{equation} \int_V \left( U\nabla^2 G - G\nabla^2 U \right) dV = \oint_S \left( U\frac{\partial G}{\partial n} - G\frac{\partial U}{\partial n} \right) dS \end{equation} and substituting the governing equations for $U$ and $G$, the left-hand side becomes: \begin{equation} \int_V \left( -U\delta(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}') + Gk^2U - Uk^2G \right) dV = -U(\mathbf{r}') \end{equation} where the $k^2$ terms cancel and the sifting property of the delta function yields $U(\mathbf{r}')$. Substituting the explicit form of $G$ with $s = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$, we arrive at Kirchhoff's integral theorem: \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r}') = \frac{1}{4\pi} \oint_S \left[ U \frac{\partial}{\partial n}\left(\frac{e^{iks}}{s}\right) - \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\frac{\partial U}{\partial n} \right] dS \label{eqn:KirchhoffIntegral} \end{equation} This is the final form of the Kirchhoff Integral theorem, with the kernel however we can recover the usual Huygens form when we consider an incident spherical wave: $U = U(\mathbf{r})=U_0\frac{e^{ikr}}{r}$, with its normal derivative: $\frac{\partial U}{\partial n} = U_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \big( \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \big) \approx ik U_0 \cos \chi \frac{e^{ikr}}{r}$ substituting these terms in gives: \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r}') = \frac{1}{4\pi} \oint_S \left[ U_0 \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} ik \cos \chi_s \frac{e^{iks}}{s} - \frac{e^{iks}}{s} ik U_0 \cos \chi_i \frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \right] dS \end{equation} \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r}') = \frac{ik}{4\pi} U_0 \oint_S \frac{e^{ik(r+s)}}{rs} \big( \cos \chi_s - \cos \chi_i \big) dS \end{equation} using the paraxial approximation to remove the $\cos \chi_s$ dependence and the original Huygens equation (\ref{eqn:huygenseqn}) is recovered. \begin{equation} \text{at P: } U(P) = -\frac{i}{d} U(r_0) \int_S \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\kappa(\chi)\, dS \tag{\ref{eqn:huygenseqn}} \end{equation} With the Green's function forward propagation appears naturally, the sum of waves at a location is amplitude weighted, boundary conditions can be easily built in, and the derivative of the field naturally yields the inclination factor $\kappa(\chi) = \frac{1}{2} (1 + \cos \chi)$, which accounts for the obliquity of the wavefront and suppresses unphysical back-propagation.\\ What can be seen in Kirchhoff's integral theorem in equation \ref{eqn:KirchhoffIntegral} is that each surface element $dS$ contributes a $\frac{e^{iks}}{s}$, a new spherical wave. There is a factor $-\frac{i}{d}$ that represents a phase delay of 90° ($-i = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}$), that gives the new wave the correct phase. \subsection{Talbot Imaging} The Talbot effect follows directly from the propagation of a periodic field, with the Helmholtz equation (\ref{eqn:helmholtzWave}).\\ \begin{equation} \nabla^2U + k^2U = 0 \tag{\ref{eqn:helmholtzWave}} \end{equation} A transmission mask with period $d$ along $y$ decomposes the incident field into the spatial harmonics of the mask: % \begin{equation} A(y, 0^+) = T(y) = \sum_n c_n\, e^{i 2\pi ny/d}. \label{eqn:grating} \end{equation} % Each harmonic, $e^{ik_y^{(n)} y}$, with $k_y^{(n)} = 2\pi n/d$, is a solution of the Helmholtz equation (\ref{eqn:helmholtzWave}) so long as: \begin{equation} k_z^{(n)} = \sqrt{k^2 - \left( \tfrac{2\pi n}{d} \right) ^2} \end{equation} % The propagator for each harmonic in the $\vec{z}$ direction is: $e^{ik_z^{(n)} z}$ % which leads to the entire field becoming: \begin{equation} A(y, z) = \sum_n c_n e^{i 2\pi ny/d} e^{iz\sqrt{k^2-(2\pi n/d)^2}} \end{equation} % The propagator can be Taylor expanded given that $\frac{2\pi n}{d} \ll k$, i.e. $d \ll d$:\\ \begin{equation} k_z^{(n)} = k\sqrt{1 - \frac{n^2d^2}{d^2}} \approx k - \frac{\pi n^2 d}{d^2}, \end{equation} and \begin{equation} A(y, z) = \sum_n c_n e^{i 2\pi ny/d} e^{ikz} e^{-iz \left( \frac{\pi n^2 d}{d^2}\right)} \label{eqn:talbotGeneral} \end{equation} % These parts of the field can now be broken down qualitatively. The term $e^{ikz}$ is the light field that varies along the propagation direction with wavelength $d$, it is quickly varying and does not affect the measurable: intensity. The remainder is the intensity pattern with $\vec{z}$. Whenever the term $e^{-iz \left( \frac{\pi n^2 d}{d^2}\right)} = 1$ the transmission mask $T(y)$ is reproduced. This re-imaging occurs whenever $z\pi n^2 \frac{d}{d^2}$ is an integer multiple of $2\pi$. $n=1$ sets the strictest condition, given $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, the first re-imaging occurs at the Talbot length $T_L$ when: \begin{equation} z = \frac{2d^2}{d} \equiv T_L \end{equation} It can also be seen that at intermediate positions, harmonics with higher $n$ may satisfy the condition while lower $n$ do not, producing partial or fractional self-images of the mask.\cite{Mansuripur_2009}\\ \subsection{Gaussian-Schell model approximation} \cite{Cai2017} \cite{Friberg1982} \cite{MandelWolf95} {\color{red}Schell model source is where the coherence function depends only on $r_2 - r_1$.} In order to move towards incoherent optics, we must introduce the mutual coherence function, and cross spectral density function. For a partially coherent field $\langle U \rangle = 0$. The spectral density is an interesting function to us, because contained within it, is the intensity of the source, and the spatial/ transverse coherence. The mutual coherence function, also contains temporal coherence.\\ We start by evaluating the electric field intensity at two points $\mathbf{r_1}$ and $\mathbf{r_2}$, the cross spectral density, $W$ is then defined as:\\ \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1}, \mathbf{r_2}) = \langle U^*(\mathbf{r_1}) U(\mathbf{r_2}) \rangle \end{equation} Where the mutual coherence function $\Gamma$ is given by:\\ \begin{equation} \Gamma(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_2},\tau) = \langle U^* (\mathbf{r_1},t)U(\mathbf{r_2},t+\tau) \rangle \label{eqn:mutualCoherence} \end{equation} To propagate this we can use the Huygens form of the Kirchhoff integral found in equation \ref{eqn:huygenseqn}: \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r'}) = \int_S K(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r'})U(\mathbf{r})dS \label{eqn:huygenseqn2} \end{equation} % Where the kernel is: $K(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r'}) = -\frac{i}{d} \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \kappa (\chi)$ % The cross-spectral density is now given by:\\ \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1'},\mathbf{r_2'}) = \iint_S K^*(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_1'})K(\mathbf{r_2},\mathbf{r_2'})W(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_2}) dS_1 dS_2 \label{eqn:crossSpectral} \end{equation} % Real sources, like thermal sources, and lasers, have intensity and coherence profiles that can be well or exactly described by Gaussians. Gaussians are also convenient in offering an analytical solution to the propagation integral. Introducing the Gaussian nature of the intensity profile and coherence function:\\ \begin{equation} I(\mathbf{r}) = I_0 e^{-\frac{r^2}{2\sigma_I^2}}, \Gamma(\mathbf{r_1}- \mathbf{r_2}) = e^{-\frac{|\mathbf{r_1}-\mathbf{r_2}|^2}{2\sigma_\Gamma^2}} \label{eqn:gaussianschellmodelsource} \end{equation} Where $\sigma_I$ and $\sigma_\Gamma$ are generally different widths of the intensity and coherence functions. Sometimes these widths are linked by the `degree of global coherence' $\beta = \frac{\sigma_\Gamma}{\sigma_I}$, which can take values from $0$ for completely incoherent, to $\infty$ for coherent beams.\\ % define Gaussian source A Gaussian Schell-model source takes the form:\\ \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r}) = U_0 \exp(-\frac{r^2}{4\sigma_I^2}) e^{i\phi(\mathbf{r})} \label{eqn:gaussianSource} \end{equation} Where $\phi(\mathbf{r})$ is a random phase, which makes $\langle U \rangle = 0$. For incoherent optics it is therefore the second order quantity, the cross-spectral density, which is generally non-zero that is interesting.\\ Using the Gaussian beam from equation \ref{eqn:gaussianSource} into the cross-spectral density in equation \ref{eqn:crossSpectral} yields:\\ \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_2}) = \langle U^*(\mathbf{r_1}) U(\mathbf{r_2}) \rangle = \sqrt{I(\mathbf{r_1}) I(\mathbf{r_2})} \Gamma (\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_2},0) \end{equation} \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1}, \mathbf{r_2}) = \sqrt{I(\mathbf{r_1}) I(\mathbf{r_2})} \Gamma(\mathbf{r_1}- \mathbf{r_2}) \label{eqn:gaussianSchellcpd} \end{equation} \subsection{van Cittert-Zernike theorem} Lets take the limits of the cross-spectral density in equation \ref{eqn:gaussianSchellcpd}. For a completely incoherent source, where $\sigma_\Gamma \rightarrow 0$ then $ W(\mathbf{r_1}, \mathbf{r_2}) = I(\mathbf{r_1}) \delta(\mathbf{r_1} - \mathbf{r_2})$. In this situation no two point, have a related phase of the light field.\\ Starting from \ref{eqn:crossSpectral}: \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1'},\mathbf{r_2'}) = \iint_S K^*(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_1'})K(\mathbf{r_2},\mathbf{r_2'})W(\mathbf{r_1},\mathbf{r_2}) dS_1 dS_2 \tag{\ref{eqn:crossSpectral}} \end{equation} \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{r_1'},\mathbf{r_2'}) = \int_S I(\mathbf{r}) K^*(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{r_1'})K(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{r_2'}) d^2\mathbf{r} \label{eqn:crossSpecIncoh} \end{equation} It is valid to assume the paraxial approximation because the source size is very small compared to the distance to the detector so the condition $|\mathbf{r_1}-\mathbf{r_2}| \ll z$. Now the paraxial kernel can be derived from the Huygens kernel. Starting with equation \ref{eqn:huygenseqn2}: \begin{equation} U(\mathbf{r'}) = \int_S K(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r'})U(\mathbf{r})dS \tag{\ref{eqn:huygenseqn2}} \end{equation} % https://dielslab.unm.edu/sites/default/files/paraxial-huygens-fresnel2.pdf\\ Where the kernel is: $K(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r'}) = -\frac{i}{d} \frac{e^{iks}}{s}\kappa (\chi)$. From the paraxial angles, $\kappa$ can immediately be set to 1, additionally the kernel can be expanded in $s$. For a source at $\mathbf{r} = (\mathbf{\rho},0)$ and observer at $\mathbf{r'} = (\mathbf{\rho'},z)$: \begin{equation} s = |\mathbf{r'}-\mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{z^2 + |\mathbf{\rho'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2} = z \sqrt{1 + \frac{|\mathbf{\rho'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2}{z^2}} \end{equation} To second order the kernel becomes: \begin{equation} K(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{r'}) = -\frac{i}{d} \frac{e^{iks}}{s} \approx -\frac{i}{d} \frac{1}{z} \exp \left( ikz + \frac{ik|\mathbf{\rho'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2}{2z} %quartic term - \frac{ik|\mathbf{\rho'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^4}{8z^3} \right) \label{eqn:fresnelKernel1} \end{equation} And cleaning up \begin{equation} K(\mathbf{\rho}, \mathbf{\rho'}) = -\frac{i}{d z} e^{ikz} \exp \left( \frac{ik|\mathbf{\rho'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2}{2z} \right) \label{eqn:fresnelKernel2} \end{equation} The conditions for this are:\\ \begin{enumerate} \item $d \ll s$ \item $\kappa (\chi) \approx 1$ \item $\frac{1}{s} \approx \frac{1}{z}$ \item The quartic term in the expansion must be allowed to be dropped, i.e. $\frac{ka^4}{8z^3} \ll 1$. This is the the Fresnel condition, it demands the paraxial approximation $\frac{a}{z} \ll 1$, and that the Fresnel number, $F = \frac{a^2}{d z} \ll 1$. \end{enumerate} Condition 2 \& 3 are both true in the paraxial approximation, demanded by condition 4. Going back to equation \ref{eqn:crossSpecIncoh}, now the Fresnel kernel can be substituted into the integral. Starting with the product:\\ \begin{equation} K^*(\boldsymbol{\rho_1},\boldsymbol{\rho_1'})K(\boldsymbol{\rho_2},\boldsymbol{\rho_2'}) = \frac{1}{(d z)^2} \exp \left( -\frac{ik}{2z}|\boldsymbol{\rho_1'} - \boldsymbol{\rho}| \right) \exp \left( \frac{ik}{2z}|\boldsymbol{\rho_2'} - \boldsymbol{\rho}| \right) \end{equation} Expanding the powers:\\ $$|\mathbf{\rho_2'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2 = \mathbf{\rho_2'}^2 - 2\mathbf{\rho_2'}\mathbf{\rho}+\mathbf{\rho}^2$$\\ $$\implies |\mathbf{\rho_2'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2 - |\mathbf{\rho_1'} - \mathbf{\rho}|^2 = \mathbf{\rho_2'}^2 - \mathbf{\rho_1'}^2 - 2\mathbf{\rho_2'}\mathbf{\rho} + 2\mathbf{\rho_1'}\mathbf{\rho}$$\\ Finally the cross-spectral density is given by: \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{\rho_1'},\mathbf{\rho_2'}) = \frac{1}{(d z)^2} \exp \left( \frac{ik}{2z} (\mathbf{\rho_2'}^2 - \mathbf{\rho_1'}^2) \right) \int_\Sigma I(\mathbf{\rho}) \exp \left( -\frac{ik}{z} (\mathbf{\rho_2'} - \mathbf{\rho_1'})\cdot \rho \right) d^2\mathbf{\rho} \label{eqn:vanCitterZernikeFull} \end{equation} Which can be recognised as a fourier transform. Finally expressing the formulation in the usual form. The van Cittert-Zernike theorem states that the mutual coherence of a source is proportional to the angular fourier transform of the source:\\ \begin{equation} W(\mathbf{\rho_1'},\mathbf{\rho_2'}) \propto \mathcal{F}\left\{I \left( \frac{\rho_2' - \rho_1'}{d z} \right) \right\} \end{equation} In terms of a Gaussian source $I(\rho) = I_0 e^{-\rho^2/2\sigma_I^2}$ this becomes:\\ \begin{equation} \mathcal{F}\left\{I \left( f \right) \right\} = 2\pi \sigma_I^2 I_0 e^{-2\pi^2 \sigma_I^2 |f|^2} \end{equation} \section{3 grating interferometers} \subsection{Moire deflectometer} In a moiré deflectometer the two gratings are separated by a distance $d \ll T_L$, so diffraction between them is negligible and the instrument operates in the geometric-optics regime. Here the light acts as rays. Qualitatively the first grating acts to produced periodic sources, and the second constrains the trajectories from these sources. A geometric pattern then appears after this second grating. We can start building the picture of the system by considering a source with angular distribution: $A(\theta)$. x_n^2 = \frac{2(m+n) \lambda z}{d}x + \frac{(m^2 - n^2) \lambda^2 z^2}{d^2} \end{equation} Substituting these relations in yields:\\ \begin{equation} W_{0}(x+\frac{m\lambda z}{d}, x - \frac{n \lambda z}{d},z=L) = I_L e^{-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}} e^{-\frac{(m - n)\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d}x} e^{-\frac{(m^2 + n^2) \lambda^2 z^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d^2}} \newline e^{-\frac{(m+n)^2\lambda^2z^2}{2\sigma^2_c(L)d^2}} \newline e^{i\frac{\pi}{R(L)\lambda}(\frac{2(m+n) \lambda z}{d}x + \frac{(m^2 - n^2) \lambda^2 z^2}{d^2})} \end{equation} \begin{equation} W_{0}(x_{s1}, x_{s2},z=L) = I_L e^{-\frac{x_{s1}^2 + x_{s2}^2}{4\sigma_I^2(L)}} e^{-\frac{(x_{s1} - x_{s2})^2}{2\sigma^2_c(L)}} e^{i\frac{k}{2R(L)}(x_{s1}^2 - x_{s2}^2)} \tag{\ref{eqn:propagatedCSD}} \end{equation} Finally, substituting this into equation \ref{eqn:propagatedCSD}, we have:\\ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} I(x, L+z) &= I_L \, e^{-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}} \sum_{m,n} a_m a_n \\[4pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ i\frac{2\pi}{d}\!\left((m-n)+\frac{(m+n)z}{R(L)}\right)x + i\pi\frac{\lambda z}{d^2}(m^2-n^2)\!\left(1+\frac{z}{R(L)}\right) \right]}_{\text{fringe phase + curvature tilt + Talbot phase}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m-n)\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d}x -\frac{(m^2+n^2)\lambda^2 z^2}{4\sigma_I^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{intensity envelope + envelope tilt}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m+n)^2\lambda^2 z^2}{2\sigma_c^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{coherence filter}} . \end{aligned} \end{equation} \newpage This analytical form is for the intensity patter after a single grating from a single GSM beam, of width the microfluidic source slits. We can now perform a summation across periodic displacement of this pattern to get a final result for our microfluidic source:\\ \begin{equation} I_\text{microfluidic} \left( x, L + z \right) = \sum_{s=-\infty}^\infty I_\text{singleSlit} \left( x - sd, L + z \right) \end{equation} There is a key observation that the fringe phase factor remains unchangded as $e^{i 2\pi (m-n) s}=1 \forall s \in \mathbb{Z}$. \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} I_\text{microfluidic}(x, L+z) &= \sum_{s} I_L \, e^{-\frac{(x-sd)^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}} \sum_{m,n} a_m a_n \\[4pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ i\frac{2\pi}{d}\!\left((m-n)+\frac{(m+n)z}{R(L)}\right)(x - sd) + i\pi\frac{\lambda z}{d^2}(m^2-n^2)\!\left(1+\frac{z}{R(L)}\right) \right]}_{\text{fringe phase + curvature tilt + Talbot phase}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m-n)\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d}(x - sd) -\frac{(m^2+n^2)\lambda^2 z^2}{4\sigma_I^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{intensity envelope + envelope tilt}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m+n)^2\lambda^2 z^2}{2\sigma_c^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{coherence filter}} . \end{aligned} \end{equation} Extracting the components that do not depend on $s$ simplifies the microfluidic intensity to:\\ \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} I_\text{microfluidic}(x, L+z) &= I_L \, \sum_{m,n} a_m a_n \\[4pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ i\frac{2\pi}{d}\!\left((m-n)+\frac{(m+n)z}{R(L)}\right)x + i\pi\frac{\lambda z}{d^2}(m^2-n^2)\!\left(1+\frac{z}{R(L)}\right) \right]}_{\text{fringe phase + curvature tilt + Talbot phase}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m-n)\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d}x -\frac{(m^2+n^2)\lambda^2 z^2}{4\sigma_I^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{intensity envelope + envelope tilt}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m+n)^2\lambda^2 z^2}{2\sigma_c^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{coherence filter}}\\[4pt] & \sum_{s} e^{-\frac{(x-sd)^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}} \exp\!\left[ i2\pi\!\left((m-n)+\frac{(m+n)z}{R(L)}\right)s \right] \exp\!\left[ -\frac{(m-n)\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}s \right] \end{aligned} \end{equation} A final simplication can be made by shift he $(m,n)$ sum into new variables $(p,q) = (m-n, m+n)$. This simplification leads to: $m^2 - n^2 = pq, m^2 + n^2 = \frac{p^2+q^2}{2}$: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} I_\text{microfluidic}(x, L+z) &= I_L \, \sum_{p,q} a_\frac{q+p}{2} a_\frac{q-p}{2} \\[4pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ i\frac{2\pi}{d}\!\left(p+\frac{qz}{R(L)}\right)x + i\pi\frac{\lambda z}{d^2}pq\!\left(1+\frac{z}{R(L)}\right) \right]}_{\text{fringe phase + curvature tilt + Talbot phase}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{p\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)d}x -\frac{(\frac{p^2+q^2}{2})\lambda^2 z^2}{4\sigma_I^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{intensity envelope + envelope tilt}} \\[6pt] &\quad \underbrace{\exp\!\left[ -\frac{q^2\lambda^2 z^2}{2\sigma_c^2(L)d^2} \right]}_{\text{coherence filter}}\\[4pt] & \sum_{s} e^{-\frac{(x-sd)^2}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}} \exp\!\left[ i2\pi\!\left(p+\frac{qz}{R(L)}\right)s \right] \exp\!\left[ -\frac{p\lambda z}{2\sigma_I^2(L)}s \right] \end{aligned} \end{equation}
